Blackhole Atomic City

The State of Decay

About us

Black Hole/Atomic City (State of Decay) slated to open at the Sanitary Tortilla Factory on August 2, 2019, is an exhibition dedicated to alternative stories related to “the nuclear business” in New Mexico since the dawn of the Anthropocene/Trinity test near Tularosa in July 1945. The combined burden of nuclear byproducts and waste that decays over tens of thousands of years weighs heavily on New Mexico, a “national sacrifice zone.” The show will be on view until August 30.

Impacts that largely affect Indigenous and rural communities from nuclear weapons and their production in New Mexico and around the world, are not featured in the celebratory story of the Atomic Age promoted by Los Alamos, set for the commemoration in 2020 of the 75th Anniversary of the Trinity Test and the bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki, the first and only cities to be destroyed with atomic weapons.
What is the impact of the theft and decimation of sacred lands? How have involuntary radiation exposures from atomic explosions and contamination from mining and milling of uranium affected generations? What are the ongoing threats from transportation and storage of radioactive waste?

This will be one of numerous efforts to highlight alternative views of our nuclear world during the lead up to the Anniversary of the Nuclear Age. The Black Hole, a Los Alamos business known world-wide for recycling equipment and materials from the Los Alamos Scientific/National Laboratory, was owned by anti-nuclear activist Edward Grothus, who died in 2009. Artist and activist Barbara Grothus is the lead organizer of the project. As we face the twin debacles of climate crisis and nuclear proliferation, both human made and life-threatening, the need for dialog and action are urgent, and we are grateful for this opportunity. Please contact Barbara Grothus for further information at bhatomiccity@gmail.com or 505-235-9529.

LOST OBJECTS

THINGS come together, some of sentimental value, some carry the past, sometimes my past, sometimes pasts of other people, places known by me and not known at all, objects that have a history with me, others in various states of wear when we meet. Something of the object brings us together. My work is with photography and video, found photographs, alternative photographic processes, and objects I collect and ceramic objects I make. This work fits within a larger, ongoing body of work, loosely called I am going to get rid of all this stuff, and the focus is on transience and the fluidity of identity. Recent bodies of work are personally transitional in that they mark my move from the UK back to my hometown of Taos. Can I ever leave my adopted home? Can I ever really come back to Taos, after 50 years? Anna Bush Crews

Project Two

Yasuyo Tanaka is a social practice artist, educator, and researcher living in NYC. Her perspective changed when she moved to the USA. The history and geography of the USA and Japan influence her work. After the Fukushima nuclear accident, nuclear and refugee problems have become her life’s work, because her family was affected by the disaster, and her hometown became the candidate to be the final disposal site for radioactive waste. She’s visited nuclear facilities and polluted areas to investigate global nuclear issues. International disputes, environmental issues, borders, discrimination, identity, media literacy, self-transformation, and all those connections are motivations for her work.

Project Three

Melanie Mills currently resides in Savannah, GA, but lived throughout New Mexico for many years. She has a busy counseling and psychotherapy private practice in Savannah, where she specializes in trauma and addictions. She studied Studio Art and Art Therapy at UNM, and was heavily influenced by the political art and murals of Mexico and New Mexico in her formative years. Mills has proclaimed her Feminist identity since the early 1970’s, and continues to explore socio-political themes in her work through sculpture and collage. Mills has exhibited work throughout the US, and has art in private collections and a public commission.
